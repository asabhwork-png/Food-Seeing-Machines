§ Abstract

Two ways of looking at the same photos

Who's Eating? lets me search and ask questions about 254 food photos I've taken over the years. It looks at them two different ways at once. One way (Route A) reads the pixels straight, using SigLIP to turn each image into a vector I can match a text query against. The other way (Route B) first asks a vision-language model, Gemma 3 4B, to write down what each photo shows in a fixed format, then searches over that text. When I ask a question, the system pulls the closest images, hands their descriptions to the model, and answers — showing me the photos it leaned on.

The reason this archive works for the assignment is simple: I took every picture, so I always know when the answer is wrong. I know which curry is Hyderabadi and which is just "Indian," which shot is a real macaron and which is a candle shaped like one. None of that is in the pixels. The caption format was the big design decision here, and I built it specifically to make the gap between what I know and what the model can see easy to see, compare, and pick apart.

§ 1 · Motivation & corpus statement

An archive I can fact-check from memory

Most people who test a retrieval system don't know the data going in, so they can't tell a good answer from a confident wrong one. I wanted the opposite. I picked a set of images I know so well that a hallucination jumps out at me instantly. Food photos are perfect for it — they're crammed with detail (ingredients, textures, plating), they carry cultural specificity that models love to guess at, and they let me set traps.

What's in the collection

254 photos I shot across two countries: home-cooked meals, restaurant food, drinks, desserts, and plenty of raw or half-made food. The India/Germany split isn't incidental — it's where the model's habit of guessing a cuisine becomes something I can actually catch and write down.

Why there are fakes in here

Mixed in with the real meals are food lookalikes and food costumes — things that look like food but aren't: a taco costume, candles moulded as macarons, a little ceramic keychain shaped like a snack. I didn't include these by accident. They're the tripwires. They're the exact spot where reading the pixels and reading the photo have to come apart, because the difference between "food" and "a picture that refers to food" lives in what you know, not in the frame. Both the caption format (§3) and the dossier (§6) are built around them.

The premise, in one line
Everything I know about a photo's origin and intent — that a shape is a tattoo, that a macaron is a candle — isn't recoverable from the pixels. So the whole project is really about giving the machine somewhere to write down what it can see, which leaves the part only I know standing out in relief.

§ 2 · Approach

How the thing is built

Route A is just my Level 1 SigLIP search, reused as-is. Route B is the new part: caption every image, embed the captions, search over those. A small keyword-boost sits on top of Route B. Both routes then feed the exact same answering step — so which route I use changes what gets found, never how the answer reads.

Grounding, and not re-running the expensive bit

When it answers, the model is told to use only the descriptions it's handed, to name images by filename, and to just say so when the descriptions don't cover the question. Captioning runs greedily so it's repeatable, and every batch gets written to Drive — captioning all 254 images is a one-time 20–45 minute GPU job, and a dropped session shouldn't cost me a thing.

§ 3 · Design decision — the caption schema & prompt

Deciding what a caption should even contain

There's no textbook "caption format for RAG" I could copy, so this was mine to figure out. I worked backwards from three things I knew I'd need later: something to search over, something that lets me compare the two routes fairly, and something I can audit when it goes wrong. That gave me ten fields:

# what I ask the model to fill in for every photo
one_sentence_summary     # plain sentence, 25 words or fewer
dish_or_subject          # the dish name if it knows it, else what's shown
dish_category            # pick one from a fixed list → lets me boost exact matches
depicts_real_food        # real_food | food_lookalike | not_food  ← the tripwire
cuisine_or_origin        # free text; 'unknown' is fine, guessing is not
preparation_stage        # raw | in_progress | ready_to_eat | not_applicable
setting                  # home | restaurant | street | studio_or_staged | unknown
key_ingredients_visible  # a list — only what you can SEE, not what you assume
colors_and_textures      # adjectives, because they're good to search on
confidence_notes         # what it's unsure about — my audit trail

The prompt — and why it's worded the way it is

The format only holds if the prompt makes it hold. I ask Gemma for JSON and nothing else, with exactly these fields. Four of the rules are doing the real work:

You are describing one photograph from a personal food-photography archive.
Return ONLY a JSON object — no markdown fences, no commentary — with exactly
these fields: { one_sentence_summary, dish_or_subject, dish_category,
depicts_real_food, cuisine_or_origin, preparation_stage, setting,
key_ingredients_visible, colors_and_textures, confidence_notes }

Rules:
- "food_lookalike" means something that resembles food but isn't edible:
  a costume, a toy, a food-shaped object.
- If people, packaging, or non-food objects fill the frame, look again at
  depicts_real_food before you answer.
- dish_category must be EXACTLY one value from the list — don't invent one.
- Describe only what you can SEE. Don't invent ingredients, places, occasions.

One small change that fixed retrieval

When I flatten a caption into a string to search over, I put the discriminating words first — category, dish name, visible ingredients, textures — and shove the prose to the end. SigLIP squashes the whole string into one vector, and the front words pull hardest. I leave confidence_notes out of the search string entirely; it's hedging for my audit trail, not a signal. Reordering this alone cleared up a drift where wordy captions kept dragging in the wrong photos.

§ 4 · Results — the atlas — ten queries, search to grounded answer

Reading the archive out loud, query by query

Ten queries run through Route A end to end — the retrieval and the grounded answer it produces. Each card shows the app's own screenshot, the answer the model wrote using only the retrieved captions, and a verdict: success (answer right, images relevant), partial (answer right but retrieval mixed, or slightly off-target), or failure (answer wrong or unhelpful).

One thing to notice across all ten: every card carries a low-confidence banner. That's not ten failures — SigLIP's text–image cosines sit structurally low (≈ 0.09–0.16), so the 0.20 warning threshold, which suits text–text scores, trips on everything. It's the same scale mismatch that shows up in §5, surfacing in the interface.

Read together, the pixel route answers look-and-feel and object-presence questions well — drinks, curry, costume, raw dough — gets vaguer when the query names a fine distinction it can't see (a "stall" versus a street meal; "fried" versus merely "crunchy"), and fails hardest exactly where the caption route has erased the thing being asked about: the not-food lookalikes. That last failure is the bridge into the dossier below.

§ 5 · Results — two routes compared — pixels vs. words

When do the pixels win, and when do the words?

Five queries run through both routes, picked because I thought they'd split the two: look-and-feel queries to Route A, name-and-knowledge queries to Route B, and one trick-shot to see which route gets fooled. What actually happened is more interesting than a clean split — Route A won all five, including the two I'd bet on Route B, and the reason turns out to be a flaw in Route B I hadn't predicted.

The tell — Route B keeps returning the same photos
Look down Route B's five result sets and the same files keep appearing. The paratha 20250306_100031 is in the top-5 of every single query; the restaurant spread IMG-20190102 and the dessert plates IMG-20210129 in four of five. These are hub images — a few captions that sit near the centre of the text-embedding space and rank highly no matter what I ask. So Route B here isn't really ranking by relevance; it's surfacing its hubs. That, not cuisine-versus-texture, is why Route A won every query in this corpus — and why my two "Route B should win" bets were wrong: dosa and raw dough turned out to have a stronger visual signature than I expected, and even where a caption did match, hubness swamped it.

One more thing the scores show: Route A's cosines sit low (≈ 0.06–0.16) while Route B's look high (≈ 0.52–0.68). That gap is a scale artefact, not a quality one — SigLIP's text–image similarities are compressed relative to its text–text similarities, so the raw numbers aren't comparable across routes. It's exactly why any later fusion of the two would need per-query normalisation before the scores mean anything together.

The tofu case — where a dumb keyword match saves the day

Caption-embedding search flat-out missed a tofu salad: the whole-string vector let "salad" drown out "tofu." So I added a small hybrid — SigLIP similarity weighted by alpha, plus the fraction of my query words that literally show up in the caption — scored across every image, so a plain "tofu" match can haul the photo back into view. It's beyond what Level 2 asks for, but I kept it because it shows the exact seam where embeddings fail and a literal word match rescues them.

§ 6 · Results — mis-seeing dossier

Three tripwires, and what they actually caught

The depicts_real_food field gets checked automatically against my own notes on which shots are fakes; any disagreement lands in the dossier. Here's the twist: only one of these three is really the model failing to see. The other two caught something I didn't expect — that my own schema wasn't fully decided. That's a design finding, not a model bug.

use–mention slip

A chili that's really a tattoo

Model saidreal_food — "a chili pepper"
Actuallynot_food — a chili tattooed on skin

Why it happened: the model isn't wrong about the object — it really is a chili. It's wrong about what that means. It collapses "a picture that refers to food" into "food is here." Its training pairs food words with food-photo pixels and barely ever sees food drawn on skin, so the classic chili shape trips the food class before the skin-and-ink context can talk it back down.

This isn't a seeing problem. The model's right — the candles aren't edible. My food_lookalike tag was carrying intent — a non-food thing pretending to be food — that just isn't in the pixels. The model has no way to read "this is imitating food on purpose." So the disagreement is really pointing at a fuzzy spot in my schema, not a fault in its vision.

A ceramic food keychain

The model reasons this one out fine — the ceramic thing imitates food, so, food_lookalike. The problem is that I called this not_food while calling the candles food_lookalike, for the exact same reason. Put the two side by side and it's obvious my not_food / food_lookalike line was never properly drawn. That's as much a finding about my schema as about the model.

The finding under the finding
Cases 2 and 3 aren't me being sloppy — they're a distinction the model simply can't make, and one I hadn't finished making either. There are two fair rules and I kept flip-flopping between them: a realism rule (how convincingly does it fake food?) and a reference rule (does it point at food at all?). Naming that split is the honest fix. A model with no read on intent was never going to settle it for me.

§ 7 · Project reflection

What I actually took away from this

The best surprise was that the schema turned into a mirror. I set out to design fields so the model could describe my food — and ended up finding the places where my own definitions didn't have an answer. The food_lookalike boundary is the clearest one: what looked like the machine's mistake was really a question I'd never sat down and decided.

The route comparison humbled me. I'd designed the five queries expecting a clean split — pixels win the look-and-feel ones, captions win the named ones — and instead Route A swept all five. The lesson wasn't "pixels are better." It was that a route can fail for a reason that has nothing to do with the axis you set out to test: Route B lost because a handful of hub captions dominate its embedding space, so it kept handing back the same paratha and dessert plates no matter what I asked. I would never have seen that from the schema or the captions in isolation; it only showed up when I lined the two routes' results side by side and noticed the repeats. That both routes sit on a SigLIP backbone and still diverge this hard is the part I keep coming back to — describing an image and then embedding the words is a genuinely different move from embedding the image, and here it introduced a failure the pixels simply don't have.

§ 8 · Limitations

What this can't do (yet)

The captions are only ever one greedy pass of a 4-bit 4B model. It still invents ingredients it's inferred from a dish name even after I've told it not to, and it states cuisine guesses with a confidence they haven't earned. Searching over a single vector per caption is coarse; the keyword boost patches it but bluntly — literal word overlap, no synonyms. Answers come from captions, so any mistake upstream slides silently into the reply. And the dossier is small on purpose: three chosen tripwires, not a survey. It shows kinds of mis-seeing, not how often they happen.

This is a Level 2 system, and it stops where Level 2 stops. It doesn't fuse the two routes into one scored number, doesn't pass images back into the model at answer time, and doesn't measure precision@k across routes. Those are the Level 3 questions — and honestly, they're where I'd go next.
